# Phonaesthetics Reference

Good UI copy doesn't just read well — it sounds good. Apply these concepts when writing new labels, CTAs, empty states, product names, and microcopy. Phonaesthetics is less relevant for error messages, which prioritize clarity over sound.

---

## Core Concepts

### Phonetic Fluency (Ease of Mouth)
The brain and mouth prefer sounds that move smoothly from one articulation point to the next.

- **Consonant-vowel alternation (CVCV)** — natural rhythm, easy to say: "Coca-Cola," "TikTok," "Luna," "Milo"
- **Avoid tongue twisters** — words requiring back-and-forth mouth gymnastics ("sixth," "rural") feel clunky and hard to process

### Euphony (Sound Harmony)
Some sound combinations are inherently pleasant because they're smooth, sonorous, or balanced.

- **Liquids and nasals (l, m, n, r, w, y)** — feel gentle and calm: "melody," "lullaby," "dream"
- **Plosives (p, b, t, d, k, g)** — add bounce or punch: "Pop-Tarts," "KitKat," "Get started"
- **Sibilants (s, sh, z)** — add softness or sensuality: "silky," "shimmer," "serene"

Mix sound types for contrast and rhythm. Going soft → hard creates balance.

### Prosody and Rhythm
Humans unconsciously crave meter and stress balance.

- **Two beats** — catchy and punchy: "Snapchat," "Netflix," "Submit," "Sign up"
- **Three beats** — melodic and memorable: "Mickey Mouse," "Coca-Cola," "Get started"
- **Irregular stress** — awkward and forgettable: "ParsnipCloud," "UploadNow"
- **Trochaic rhythm** (stress-unstress) hits hard then glides: "Apple," "Google," "Spotify"

### Phonaesthesia (Sound Symbolism)
Certain sound clusters carry subconscious emotional or sensory meaning.

- **gl-** → light, vision, clarity (glow, glimmer, glitter)
- **sn-** → nasal, unpleasant (sniff, snore, snot)
- **sl-** → slippery, sly (slide, slip, slick)
- **gr-** → heavy, rough (grind, growl, grunt)

## Quick Rules for UX Copy

- Double syllables feel friendly and catchy
- Trochaic stress (stress-unstress) sounds natural in English
- Short is punchy; long is elegant
- Alliteration and rhyme help phrases stick in memory
- Soft vowels and liquids (l, m, n, r, w, y) for calm and elegant contexts
- Hard plosives (p, b, t, d, k, g) for energetic and bold contexts
- Mixed textures (soft → hard) create balance
